In [[/blog/2018/09/29/writing-a-programmers-editor-part-9/][Part 9]] we will have syntax highlighting. The editor is starting to look and feel real.
But one thing is missing: the ability to /take back/ a mistake. Every editor needs undo.

This part covers:

- *Linear undo:* a simple stack of operations, push on edit, pop on undo.
- *The command pattern:* representing each edit as a reversible object (do/undo pair).
- *Grouping:* why typing a word is one undo, not one per character.
- *Undo trees:* vim's approach — branching history where you can undo to any past state
  and then go in a different direction.
- Emacs' undo: the "no redo, just undo the undo" philosophy.
- Implementing a command log with undo/redo in Scheme.

Undo is the safety net. Get it wrong and users lose trust in the editor. Get it right and
they will experiment freely, knowing they can always go back.

Watchout for this assay when it is published, till then.
